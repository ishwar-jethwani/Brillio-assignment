## 1. Topic / Theme Categorisation

**Approach — hybrid, rule-based over upstream signals.** Every transcript already ships with a model-generated `topics` array and typed `keyMoments`. Rather than spend a second LLM pass (slower, non-deterministic, harder to defend to a leadership panel), I map those signals into a fixed, human-named taxonomy with a transparent keyword rule set. It is auditable, instant, and reproducible — and it degrades gracefully (`Other / Uncategorised`) when nothing matches. A clustering/LLM pass is the natural next step once the taxonomy needs to discover *new* themes rather than sort into known ones.


**Call-type inference.** The raw data has no call-type field, so I derive it: a `Support Case #…` title ⇒ **support**; any non-Aegis email domain in the room ⇒ **external** (customer present); everyone on `aegiscloud.com` ⇒ **internal**.

## 2. Sentiment Analysis Across Call Types

| Call type | Calls | Avg score (1–5) | Avg negativity ratio | % calls w/ churn signal |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| external | 43 | 3.71 | 0.14 | 48.8% |
| internal | 30 | 3.42 | 0.17 | 40.0% |
| support | 27 | 2.94 | 0.23 | 70.4% |

**What the trend means.**

- **Support calls are the emotional low point** (avg 2.94/5, churn signal in 70.4% of calls). These are the conversations where customers are actively frustrated — they are the leading indicator of revenue at risk, not a lagging support metric.
- **External calls sit highest** (avg 3.71/5). 
- The gap between the most-negative and most-positive call type is the single most useful operational signal here: it tells leadership *where the customer pain is concentrated* and therefore where a CS / reliability intervention buys the most retention.

> **Why care:** sentiment on its own is a vanity chart. Tied to call type and to churn signals, it becomes a prioritisation tool — it ranks which accounts and which workflows to fix first.

### 3.4 Action-Item Accountability Tracker (every leader)

Every call carries explicit `actionItems` with named owners. Extracting and grouping them by owner/account creates a cross-call commitment ledger — who promised what, and whether the next call references it being done. This is a lightweight way to measure follow-through, which is invisible in any single transcript.


## 4. Method, Caveats & Next Steps

- **Reproducibility:** stdlib-only, deterministic — `python pipeline.py` regenerates `results.json`, `insights.md`, and `report.html` identically.

- **Trust the upstream labels, but verify:** sentiment and key-moment types are model-generated. I treat them as features, not ground truth; the next step is a spot-check sample to estimate label precision before acting on the churn board.

- **Scaling:** the rule-based taxonomy is a starting point. At 10k+ transcripts I would add embedding clustering to surface *emergent* themes the fixed taxonomy can't name, then fold the good clusters back into the rules.
